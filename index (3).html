Aelthian /æl.θi.ən/

Originated in Northern Europe, circa 8th Century BCE

Language Family:
Indo-European (Northern Branch)
Writing System:
Alphabetic (Mixed Runic-Latin)
Key Features:
Tonal, Agglutinative, Consonant-Heavy
Word Order:
Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)
Grammatical Classes:
Three classes (animate, inanimate, divine)
Current Status:
Endangered, with approximately 2,300 speakers remaining

Language Synopsis

Aelthian emerged as a distinct language around the 8th century BCE in coastal regions of Northern Europe. It developed from Proto-Germanic roots but with significant influence from now-extinct Finnic languages of the region. The language features a complex consonant system with unique sounds not found in related languages, as well as a tonal system likely influenced by contact with merchant traders from distant lands.

Historically spoken across northern coastal communities, Aelthian reached its peak distribution during the 5th-3rd centuries BCE when its speakers controlled important amber trade routes. Following the Roman expansions and later Germanic migrations, the language gradually lost speakers, surviving today primarily in isolated fishing communities and among scholars dedicated to preserving this unique linguistic heritage.
